# Tibetan / Buddhist Astrology — interpretation guide

A hybrid system (ref/1.txt: "Indian/Chinese" blend) used across Himalayan Buddhist
cultures — Tibet, Bhutan, Mongolia, parts of Nepal and Ladakh. It draws from:
1. **Tibetan elemental astrology** (Jyung-khor) — the same five elements as Chinese BaZi
   but applied through a distinct 12-year animal cycle and 60-year sexagenary cycle.
2. **Kalachakra** — "Wheel of Time," an advanced Buddhist tantric system giving cosmic
   timing, element cycles, and detailed longevity/health analysis.
3. **Mewa (sMe-ba)** — a nine-number magic square (derived from the Lo Shu) that cycles
   yearly, monthly, and daily; each person has a natal Mewa that shapes fortune and risk.
4. **Parkha (sPar-kha)** — eight trigrams (from the I Ching/Ba Gua) adapted into a Tibetan
   framework of elemental directions and life-domain fortunes.

**No engine computation** — the Tibetan calendar (lunisolar with intercalary months) is
not supported by the current engine. Use this reference for qualitative guidance when a
user asks about Tibetan/Buddhist astrology, their Losar year sign, or Kalachakra context.

## The Twelve Animals & Five Elements (same cycle as BaZi, shifted by 1 year)

The Tibetan year cycle follows the same 12 animals (Rat, Ox, Tiger, Rabbit, Dragon,
Snake, Horse, Sheep/Goat, Monkey, Bird/Rooster, Dog, Pig) and five elements (Wood,
Fire, Earth, Iron/Metal, Water), but the **Tibetan Losar (New Year)** falls in
February–March (the same Lunar New Year as Chinese). The animal and element are
therefore the same as the Chinese BaZi year animal — **use the BaZi engine's year
pillar as the Tibetan year animal** (they share the sexagenary cycle base).

## The Nine Mewa (fate numbers)

Each person has a natal Mewa = (birth year's Mewa number), calculated as:
- Start at Mewa 1 for the Rabjung 17 cycle year 2027 (Water Rabbit, female baseline).
  The Mewa descends by 1 each year for females and ascends by 1 for males (cycling 1–9).
- The nine Mewas in the Lo Shu grid:

| Mewa | Name | Element | Fortune tendency |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | White Mewa | Water | Intelligence, travel, adaptability; watch deceit |
| 2 | Black Mewa | Earth | Stability, family bonds, hard work; watch overwork |
| 3 | Indigo Mewa | Water | Ambition, litigation, risk-taking; watch conflict |
| 4 | Green Mewa | Wood | Growth, creativity, travel, arts; watch instability |
| 5 | Yellow Mewa | Earth | Power, leadership, transformation, centre; double-edged |
| 6 | White Mewa | Metal | Authority, analysis, righteousness; watch rigidity |
| 7 | Red Mewa | Metal | Joy, speech, charisma; watch gossip and superficiality |
| 8 | White Mewa | Earth | Mountains, stillness, wealth accumulation; watch stubbornness |
| 9 | Maroon Mewa | Fire | Recognition, passion, brilliance; watch impulsiveness |

The **annual Mewa** (current year's number in the Lo Shu) interacts with the person's
natal Mewa: when they share the same number it's a year of doubled intensity (good or
bad, depending on the Mewa's nature). When the annual Mewa is the element that controls
the natal Mewa, it's a difficult year calling for caution.

## Parkha trigrams (sPar-kha)

Eight trigrams (like Ba Gua) are assigned at birth based on gender and year:
- **Male Parkha** (Khön direction): Khen (sky/father), Kham (water/danger), Gön (thunder/eldest son), Zön (wind/eldest daughter), Khe (fire/middle daughter), Ön (mountain/youngest son), Dha (earth/mother), Dha (lake/youngest daughter)
- Each Parkha corresponds to a life direction (good/bad directions for travel, home positioning), auspicious and inauspicious years, and compatibility with partners.

In practice: if a user mentions Tibetan Feng Shui (Gyalshi) or directional placement,
the Parkha determines auspicious compass directions for sleep, desk, entry, etc.

## Kalachakra timing framework

Kalachakra operates on:
- A **60-year outer cycle** (matching the sexagenary cycle of BaZi).
- A **12-year inner cycle** of elemental lung-ta (wind-horse / life force).
- A personal **vitality (srog), body (lus), power (wangthang), and luck (lungta)** assessment
  based on the year animal's relationship to the current year.

**Practical Kalachakra for readings**:
- **Lung-ta (wind-horse / life luck)** — the current year's element relative to the birth
  year element. If the year element **generates** the birth year element → wind-horse rises
  (good fortune, positive momentum). If it **controls** the birth year element → challenging
  year. Same element → neutral, steady.
- **La (soul/spirit)** year — every 12 years, when the same animal recurs, is a year of
  heightened spiritual significance and personal vulnerability — similar to the Chinese
  Ben Ming Nian. Precautions: wear protective amulets, do pujas, engage in merit-making.

## Buddhist ethical frame for readings

Tibetan astrology is embedded in Dharma. Key differences from Western/Chinese framing:

- **Karma and interdependence** — difficult periods are understood as karma ripening, not
  punishment. The purpose of knowing is to **accumulate merit** (generosity, prayer, mantras)
  to mitigate the negative and amplify the positive.
- **Remedial practices (Chö-pa)** — standard recommendations include: reciting specific
  mantras (Om Mani Padme Hum for general protection), performing smoke offerings (sang),
  offering butter lamps, and having monks perform specific pujas (Tara puja for obstacles,
  Medicine Buddha for health).
- **Not fate** — even more than in Western astrology, Tibetan astrology emphasises that
  the chart shows tendencies and timings, not fixed outcomes. Compassionate action and
  spiritual practice can always shift the pattern.
- **Death timing** — classical texts do discuss longevity calculations. Never use this to
  predict death for a client. If a user asks about death timing, redirect to practices
  that promote longevity and a meaningful life.

## Reading recipe (when a user asks about Tibetan/Buddhist astrology)

1. Identify their birth year animal and element (same as BaZi — use the engine's year
   pillar output directly).
2. Give the Tibetan framing of that animal and element's character qualities.
3. State the current year's relationship to their birth year (Lung-ta rising, stable, or
   challenged) and what it means practically.
4. If they know their Mewa number, interpret it qualitatively.
5. Offer Buddhist framing: the difficulty or fortune as karma, and 1-2 merit-making
   practices aligned to the pattern (e.g. Tara practice for a challenging year).
6. Close with agency — Buddhism is fundamentally about the power of intention and action
   to shape experience. Never leave someone feeling fated.
